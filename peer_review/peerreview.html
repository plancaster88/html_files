Analytics Peer Review

This document will give an overview of why and how to have a peer review process in place for analytics teams.

Why have a peer review process?
  1. It gives all individuals involved in peer review an opportunity to see what other peers are working on along with their methods for acquiring, transforming, analyzing, and presenting data.
    • Pick up new coding/tech specific tips and tricks
    • Learn where peers are SMEs
    • Allows us to answer why numbers are different for similar projects
  2. It gives us an opportunity to identify potential data errors/discrepancies/mistakes in a project.
    • Data coming from our departments will have a greater chance of being correct. - obvious but important!
    • There is a potential that individuals may put more effort into verifying their work if they know others will be peer reviewing.
  3. Others may have suggestions for improvement.

Steps for peer review.
  1. Identify if a task/project needs to go through the peer review process
    • Probably - If you are writing new code with new logic.
    • Probably - If you are sending an analysis to someone to support an operation or decision.
    • Probably - Feeling like a 2nd set of eyes could help.
    • Probably Not - A routine process that already went through peer review (unless there is logic or structure change)
  2. Identify who needs to peer review and send your work/output/code with notes on request specifics.
    • Managers usually only need to be included for crucial projects that they are responsible for.
    • Notes should be a quick summary.
    • Indicate how soon you need the peer review to be complete.
  3. Implement necessary changes identified in peer review.
    • Correct data errors
    • Suggestions for change can be made at your discretion. Note: Many times, suggestions from others are personal preference and do not necessitate an update.

For peer reviewers.
  • Most of the time there will only need to be 1 person peer reviewing (unless it is a crucial project).
  • Only offer to peer review if you can complete the peer review in the requested time.
  • Most peer reviews shouldn't take more than 30 minutes. 15-20 minutes is probably normal. By all means - spend more time if you are really wanting a deeper understanding of the data/request.
  • Feel free to peer review even if someone else called it (if you have time). However, try not to duplicate efforts.

Example of a peer review email.

Email Subject: Peer Review | Urgent Care Utilization 2018

I'd like a peer review for a project that Mark gave me where he wants to see Urgent Care Utilization for 2018 dates of service. SQL and Excel output have been attached.

Specs:

  • 2018 dates of service
  • Only paid and final claims
  • Show visits (defined as a distinct member and date of service), total costs, total cost per visit, utilizing members (UM), total cost per UM.
  • Break this out by regions 1-9 and group provider.

Mark needs this info to inform a decision about renegotiating a contract w/ ABC Urgent Care Center. Peer review needed by end of day so I can send sometime before noon tomorrow.
